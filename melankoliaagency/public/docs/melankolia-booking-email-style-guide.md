# Melankolia Booking Email Style Guide

Source: sampled Gmail Sent messages from `booking@melankoliaagency.com` after read-only authorization. Use this guide for Route Planner, Venue Finder, Advancing, and Email Generator copy. Do not quote private email content verbatim unless explicitly approved.

## Voice

Melankolia booking emails are warm, direct, practical, and human. They are not overly corporate. The tone is polite and relationship-forward, usually written as Anna-Maria / Melankolia rather than a generic agency robot.

Common feel:
- Friendly but businesslike
- Respectful of the promoter/venue’s schedule and local scene
- Confident about the artist without sounding like inflated marketing copy
- Practical about routing, dates, availability, fees, advance details, and links
- Relationship-aware: references prior events, festivals, weekends, or previous hosting when relevant

## Target Sound: First-Touch “Booking inquiry” Emails

Adrian specifically identified the first-touch sent emails with subjects like `Booking inquiry: ...` as the tone to target.

These are not ultra-short emails. In the sampled sent corpus, first-touch “Booking inquiry” emails are usually substantial but still readable: roughly 220–760 words, often centered around 450–550 words when full artist context and links are included.

### Subject Pattern

Use a clear, searchable booking subject:

- `Booking inquiry: [genre/descriptor] [Artist] ([country]) to play [City] in [Month/Year]`
- `Booking inquiry: [Artist A] + [Artist B] to play [City] in [Month/Year]`
- `Booking inquiry: [genre/descriptor] [Artist] to play [Venue/City] on [date]`

Common descriptors are direct and genre-specific:
- dark pop artist
- dark electronic duo
- darkwave legend
- darkwave / dark electronic legend
- synth-pop act
- EBM-hammer
- post-punk / darkwave act

### Opening Pattern

Use a named greeting whenever possible:

- `Hello [Name],`
- `Hello [Venue] booking,`
- `Hello [Name] and all,`
- `Hei [Name],` / `Moikka [Name],` for Finnish contacts

Then use a warm, human bridge before the ask:

- `I hope all is well!`
- `I hope you had a great and successful [festival/weekend/event]!`
- `Thank you again for hosting our artists in [city].`
- `I wanted to reach out because we would love to bring [artist] to [city].`
- `I wanted to check with you about another booking as [artist] is returning to [region] in [month].`

### First Paragraph Formula

The first paragraph should very quickly answer:

1. Who is the artist?
2. Why are we contacting this venue/promoter?
3. What city/date window are we asking about?
4. Is this a rare/routed/opportunistic date?
5. Could they host, or add the artist to an existing event?

Example structure to emulate, not quote verbatim:

`I hope all is well! I wanted to reach out because we would love to bring [Artist] to [City] while they are routing through [region] in [month]. We have [date/date window] available. Would you have availability to host them then? They could also be added to an already existing event if you have something scheduled around that date.`

### Artist Context Section

After the ask, include 2–4 compact paragraphs on the artist:

- one identity sentence: name, origin, project/person if useful
- one sound/cultural positioning paragraph
- one credibility paragraph: albums, festivals, tours, publications, collaborations, audience metrics
- one live-show/tour-specific note if relevant

The tone should be confident but grounded. Avoid generic hype like “unforgettable experience” unless supported by specific context.

Good ingredient types:
- album/release title and year
- recognizable remixers/collaborators
- festival appearances
- previous successful tours
- press mentions
- Spotify monthly listeners / Instagram following if relevant
- “rare opportunity” framing when visas/routing make the date meaningful

### Links Section

Use a simple links block after the artist context:

`You can find their socials and EPK below:`

Then include:
- Spotify
- Instagram
- Bandcamp
- recent music video
- EPK

For specific artists, include the most relevant signature video or current release, not a random link dump.

### CTA / Close

Close with a soft but clear booking ask:

- `Let me know if you are interested in booking them and I would be happy to discuss the details.`
- `Let me know if you would like to book [Artist] and we can discuss the details.`
- `Would you have availability to host them then?`
- `They could also be added to an already existing event if you have something scheduled then.`
- `I look forward to hearing your thoughts!`
- `I look forward to hearing from you!`

Default signoff:

`Best wishes,`

`Anna-Maria`

## Broader Reply / Follow-Up Tone

Replies and logistics emails are shorter and more practical than first-touch “Booking inquiry” emails.

Common reply patterns:
- `Nice to hear from you again!`
- `Thank you for your email and for sharing your perspective.`
- `Could you please send the advance details for today?`
- `Which payment method do you prefer to use?`
- `Let me know which option works best.`

## Practical Rules for Generated Emails

- For first-touch venue/promoter outreach, target the “Booking inquiry” structure above, not a short generic pitch.
- Default first-touch length: 350–550 words when artist context/links are available.
- Default reply length: 80–220 words.
- Lead with date/city/routing relevance early; do not bury the actual ask.
- Always include a specific booking question, not just a general introduction.
- Use “Hello [Name],” unless a regional/local greeting is more natural.
- Mention relationship context when known: previous hosting, festival, local scene, shared artist, or prior exchange.
- Use “Best wishes, Anna-Maria” unless explicitly told otherwise.
- Include EPK/social/music links as a neat block.
- Avoid over-polished corporate phrasing. It should sound like a real agent reaching out with a concrete opportunity.
